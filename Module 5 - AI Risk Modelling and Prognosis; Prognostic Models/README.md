## AI Risk Modelling and Prognosis
Prognostic models in healthcare are built to do one thing well: predict who is at risk and by how much. The accuracy of those predictions determines whether clinical resources reach the right patients at the right time. This module focused on how that accuracy gets measured and where the measurement itself can mislead. The central concept was Net Reclassification Improvement, a metric used to evaluate whether a new or enhanced model does a meaningfully better job of sorting patients into correct risk categories than a baseline model does.

NRI works by examining two patient groups separately. For patients who do not have the disease, it measures how many were correctly moved to lower risk categories. For patients who do have the disease, it measures how many were correctly moved to higher risk categories. Those two values combine into a total NRI score that speaks to whether adding new variables to a model produced genuine clinical improvement. What makes NRI valuable is that it goes beyond broad accuracy measures and asks a more pointed question: did the model's predictions actually change in the right direction for the right patients. The limitation worth taking seriously is that NRI can register improvement even when a random variable is added to the model. That means reclassification gains can reflect statistical noise rather than true predictive value, which is why NRI should always be paired with additional evaluation methods before its results are trusted in clinical practice.
The second part of this module extended that skepticism to a broader problem: where prognostic models are developed and where they are actually used. Most models are built using data from high-income countries, then transferred to low and middle income settings where the populations, disease burdens, healthcare infrastructure, and available diagnostics look fundamentally different. A systematic review found only 21 prognostic models developed or validated in LMIC settings, and of those, only one had undergone external validation. One out of twenty-one. That figure points directly to what researchers call the 10/90 gap, where 10% of global research resources are directed at conditions that account for 90% of the global disease burden.

The consequences of that gap are not abstract. Over 95% of maternal and perinatal deaths occur in LMIC settings, which are the exact environments where validated models are most scarce. A model imported from a high-income context and applied without validation in a different population does not simply underperform. It can actively misclassify risk, direct resources to the wrong patients, and miss the ones who need urgent intervention. This module made the case that model performance cannot be separated from the context it was built in, and that validation in the target population is a clinical necessity, not a methodological preference.
