## AI-Based Diagnostic Tools
Standard HIV testing has a fundamental limitation. The human body takes weeks to produce detectable antibodies after infection, which means routine tests return negative results during the window when transmission risk is highest. The Determine™ HIV Early Detect test was designed to close that gap by detecting both antibodies and the p24 antigen, a protein that appears in the blood significantly earlier during acute HIV infection. This assignment evaluated how the test performed when deployed in real clinical settings across rural Eswatini, a country where more than one in four adults live with HIV, and whether its concrete results held up against its laboratory promise.

Understanding the test's performance required working through two core diagnostic concepts. Sensitivity measures how reliably a test identifies people who are actually infected. Low sensitivity means infections are being missed. Specificity measures how reliably a test clears people who are not infected. Low specificity means healthy people are being incorrectly flagged. The Determine™ test posted a specificity of 99.8%, meaning a negative result could be trusted with high confidence. For detecting established HIV infection, sensitivity came in at 83.7%. For acute HIV infection, the number the entire test was built around, sensitivity dropped to 20%.

Eight out of ten people with acute HIV infection were missed. The study identified several explanations. Some participants were likely tested in the earliest days of infection before p24 levels were high enough to register. Others were probably in a biological window where emerging antibodies bind to the antigen and effectively conceal it from detection. Fingerprick blood samples, which are less concentrated than the plasma used in controlled lab studies, further reduced the test's ability to capture low antigen levels. Field conditions including heat, humidity, and variability between testers added additional noise that controlled studies cannot account for.

For detecting established HIV infection the test is reliable, practical, and well suited for resource-limited settings. That part works. The problem is that detecting established infection was never the point. The entire value proposition of this test was its ability to catch HIV during the acute phase when viral loads are highest and transmission is most likely. A 20% sensitivity for that use case is not a marginal shortfall, it is a failure of the core objective. This assignment reinforced a critical principle in healthcare AI and diagnostics: strong laboratory performance does not guarantee real-world effectiveness, and field evaluations are not optional, they are where the truth lives.
