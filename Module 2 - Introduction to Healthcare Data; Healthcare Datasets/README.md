## Introduction to Healthcare Datasets
Healthcare data is not clean by default. It comes in varying formats, across inconsistent structures, and at a scale that makes manual analysis impractical. This module introduced the realities of working with healthcare datasets by presenting a concrete analytical problem: given a mock outpatient clinic dataset containing patient demographics, visit information, and health metrics, calculate the average age and average heart rate across the full patient population. The goal was to understand how raw healthcare data gets transformed into meaningful statistical output.

The analysis started with the underlying math. The formula for calculating an average is the sum of all values divided by the total number of records, a simple operation that becomes unmanageable at scale without the right tools. A manual example was worked through first to establish that conceptual foundation, then Python was applied to the full dataset. Using a Jupyter Notebook, the pandas library was used to load the data via read_csv(), compute averages with the .mean() function, and display results rounded to two decimal places. This workflow mirrors how healthcare analysts approach real datasets where thousands of records make anything other than a programmatic solution unrealistic.

The results showed an average patient age of 52 years and an average heart rate of 80.0 bpm. Those numbers on their own are a snapshot of the population, but the more important takeaway was methodological. Knowing how to move from a raw CSV file to a clean statistical output is a foundational skill in healthcare analytics. The same process used here to calculate two averages scales directly to the kind of population-level analysis that informs clinical decisions, resource allocation, and patient risk stratification.
